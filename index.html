Venezuelan Recipes

Venezuelan gastronomy is the result of a collision of different cultures originating from Europe and parts of Africa.

Next, we will explore some of the most typical dishes of Venezuelan culture.

Cachapa

This typical dish originates from the indigenous people who inhabited Venezuelan territory and revered corn religiously. The cachapa is a type of pancake made with corn, milk or water, sugar, and salt. It has a sweet flavor and is usually served with margarine or butter and the typical cheese from the Llanos region.

Ingredients:
  • 4 cups of tender corn kernels
  • 1 cup of milk
  • 2 tablespoons of sugar
  • 1 tablespoon of salt
  • 1/4 cup of melted butter
  • Grated fresh cheese, preferably Venezuelan “queso de mano”
  • For cooking, you’ll need a little butter or oil to grease the grill.
  1. If you’re using fresh tender corn, remove the kernels from the cobs until you have the required 4 cups. If you opt for canned tender corn, make sure to drain it and rinse it thoroughly under cold water.
  2. In a blender, mix the corn kernels with milk, sugar, and salt. Blend until you achieve a smooth and homogeneous mixture.
  3. Next, transfer this corn mixture to a bowl and add the melted butter. Mix everything thoroughly to integrate the ingredients.
  4. To cook the cachapas, heat a grill or non-stick skillet over medium-high heat and lightly grease it with butter or oil.
  5. Now, add a portion of the cachapa mixture onto the hot grill to form a circle approximately 10-12 cm in diameter.
  6. Cook the cachapas until they are golden on the bottom and you see bubbles forming on the surface, which should take about 3-4 minutes.
  7. Carefully flip the cachapas and cook the other side until it is golden and fully cooked, which will take another 2-3 minutes.
  8. Finally, serve the hot cachapas and don’t forget to sprinkle grated fresh cheese on top for that characteristic and delicious touch..

Empanada

The antiquity of empanadas is as ancient as bread itself, with origins traced back to Mesopotamia. This assertion is supported by the oldest known recipe book, dating back to 1700 BCE.

Ingredients

For the dough:

  • 250g of corn flour
  • 1g of salt
  • 1g of sugar
  • 50g of wheat flour
  • 2dl of water (or as needed)

For the filling:

  • 1kg of shark (cazón)
  • 2 onions
  • Sweet paprika
  • Salt/li>
  • Pepper/li>
  • Cilantro
  1. In a pan, sauté the seasoned onion until it becomes translucent. Add the diced shark meat(cazón) and cook until it takes on color.
  2. Season with salt, pepper, and chopped cilantro.
  3. In a bowl, mix the precooked corn flour, all-purpose flour, sugar, and a pinch of salt. Gradually add water and knead until you have a manageable dough. Let it rest for 30 minutes.
  4. Roll out the dough and cut it into circles. Fill each circle with the shark mixture and fold it into a half-moon shape.
  5. Heat vegetable oil in a deep pan. Fry the empanadas until golden brown.
  6. Serve the cazón empanadas hot

pabellón

The pabellón criollo is one of the most emblematic dishes in Venezuelan cuisine, and its origin dates back to the colonial era. During Spanish colonization, Venezuela was one of the primary regions for meat and livestock production in Latin America.

Ingredients
  • 1/2kg of black beanss
  • 2 medium onions
  • Sweet, green, and red peppers
  • 1 head of garlic
  • 1/2 tablespoon of black pepper
  • 1/2 tablespoon of ground oregano
  • 1 stalk of green onion
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar
  • 1/2kg of beef skirt steak or round roast (muchacho redondo)
  • 1/2 cups of rice
  • Salt to taste
  • 1/2 cup of annatto-infused oil (aceite onotado)
  • 2 ripe plantains
  1. Soak the black beans for approximately 4 hours. Then, wash them and cook them in enough water until they become tender.
  2. Separately, simmer the minced meat in pieces until it’s tender. Remove it from the broth and let it rest so you can shred it with your fingers (reserve the broth).
  3. Finely chop allEn the seasonings and sauté them with annatto-infused oil, crushed garlic, black pepper, and ground oregano in a medium-sized skillet or pot. Set aside 2 or 3 tablespoons of the sautéed seasonings and add them to the softened black beans along with sugar and salt to taste. Let the beans cook for about 15 more minutes without drying out.
  4. In the same skillet or pot where you have the remaining seasonings, add the shredded meat. Fry it briefly, stirring to mix it with the seasonings, and then add a cup of the broth from cooking the meat. Season with salt to taste and let it cook until all the broth is absorbed.
  5. In a medium-sized pot, heat 2 cups of the remaining broth. Add salt to taste and the rice. Cook until it reaches the desired consistency.
  6. Separately, in a frying pan, heat oil and fry the sliced ripe plantains.
  7. Serve as shown in the presentation.
