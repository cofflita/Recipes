Arepa

“The arepa dates back approximately 900 years,” said Venezuelan writer Ricardo Estrada Cuevas to CNN. He is the author of the book Arepólogo, and he pointed out a time when neither of the two countries had been founded yet. “It doesn’t belong exclusively to Colombia or Venezuela. It was consumed in this region of the Americas, in both regions”

ingredients

  • 1 cup of white or yellow pre-cooked cornmeal (the easiest to find in most large supermarkets is the P.A.N. brand). Although the result won’t be the same, you can make them with regular cornmeal (Maicena); they’ll lose some flavor, but it’s much easier to find.
  • Double the quantity (2 cups of the same measurement) with water.
  • A level teaspoon of fine salt.

Preparation

  1. In a bowl, combine the cornmeal and salt. Gradually add water until a smooth dough forms that doesn’t stick to your hands. The amount of water needed depends on the type of cornmeal; this method ensures the right consistency. If you prefer, you can substitute some of the water with a little milk.
  2. Shape the dough into medium-sized balls (similar to tennis balls) and flatten them until they are about 2 cm thick.
  3. Heat a skillet with a little oil over low to medium heat. Cook the arepas on both sides until they are well-cooked inside and easily release from the pan.
  4. For a slight puffiness and a hollow interior, you can finish cooking the arepas in the oven. Preheat the oven to 180°C (350°F) and bake them for about 10 minutes.
  5. Alternatively, you can fry the arepas in oil. Surprisingly, they absorb very little oil. Fry them until they are golden brown, then fill them with your desired ingredients.
